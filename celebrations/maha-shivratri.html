Mahashivratri

The Sacred Night of Shiva–Parvati Vivah

Mahashivratri, meaning “The Great Night of Shiva”, is one of the most sacred festivals in Hindu tradition. It is primarily celebrated as the divine marriage of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati, symbolizing the union of consciousness (Shiva) and energy (Shakti).

The festival is observed on the 14th night of the waning moon in the month of Phalguna. In 2026, Mahashivratri falls on Sunday, 15 February, with the most auspicious Nishita Kaal Puja from 11:55 PM to 12:56 AM.

The Story of Shiva and Parvati’s Marriage

According to the Shiva Purana and Skanda Purana, Goddess Parvati undertook severe penance to win Lord Shiva as her husband. Shiva, the supreme ascetic, was immersed in deep meditation, detached from worldly life.

Parvati’s unwavering devotion, discipline, and purity ultimately moved Shiva. Their marriage on Mahashivratri represents the balance of renunciation and responsibility, stillness and movement, wisdom and love.

This sacred union is celebrated across India, especially by married women and young girls, who pray for marital harmony, inner strength, and spiritual growth.

The Deeper Spiritual Meaning

Mahashivratri is not merely about rituals, but about aligning one’s inner energies. Shiva represents pure awareness, while Parvati represents dynamic power. Their union signifies the completion of life.

Spiritually, this night is considered powerful because natural planetary positions are believed to support the upward movement of energy within the human body. This is why staying awake (Jagran) with awareness is emphasized.

Other Sacred Beliefs Associated with Mahashivratri

While Shiva–Parvati Vivah is the primary belief, scriptures also associate Mahashivratri with other profound events.

  • Samudra Manthan: Shiva consumed the deadly poison Halahala to save the universe, earning the name Neelkanth.
  • Shiva Linga Manifestation: Some traditions believe Shiva revealed himself as the infinite Jyotirlinga on this night.

These interpretations do not contradict each other, but together reflect Shiva’s role as protector, ascetic, and cosmic balance.

Rituals and Sacred Practices

Abhishek of Shiva Linga

The Shiva Linga is bathed with water, milk, honey, and Panchamrit. This symbolizes purification of the self and calming of Shiva’s intense cosmic energy.

Jagran and Chanting

Devotees stay awake all night, chanting “Om Namah Shivaya”, singing bhajans, and meditating. Awareness through the night is believed to accelerate spiritual growth.

Fasting and Sacred Food (Vrat)

Fasting on Mahashivratri is a discipline of body and mind.

  • Nirjala Vrat: Complete fast
  • Phalahari: Fruits, milk, non-grain food
  • Vrat Dishes: Sabudana Khichdi, Kuttu Puri, sweet potato

Lord Shiva is offered Bilva leaves, Panchamrit, and seasonal fruits. Thandai is traditionally consumed in many regions.

Traditional Attire

Simplicity and purity define Mahashivratri clothing.

  • Colours: Green, white, yellow
  • Men: Dhoti-Kurta or cotton Kurta-Pajama
  • Women: Saree or Salwar Kameez
  • Accessories: Rudraksha beads

Black and dark blue colours are traditionally avoided.

Marathi Mantra Chanted on Mahashivratri

ॐ नमः शिवाय ।
शिव-पार्वती विवाह मंगलमय होवो,
जीवनात शांती, भक्ती आणि संतुलन लाभो ॥

(Meaning: Salutations to Lord Shiva. May the sacred union of Shiva and Parvati bless our lives with peace, devotion, and balance.)

Mahashivratri is a night of union — of soul and energy, stillness and movement, devotion and wisdom. By observing this sacred night with awareness and discipline, devotees seek harmony within and blessings for a balanced life.
