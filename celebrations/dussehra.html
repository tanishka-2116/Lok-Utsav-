Dussehra / Vijayadashami

The Triumph of Dharma over Adharma

Dussehra, also known as Vijayadashami, is one of the most important Hindu festivals, symbolising the eternal victory of good over evil. It is celebrated on the tenth day after Navratri, marking the culmination of nine nights of devotion and discipline.

The festival commemorates two major victories: Lord Rama’s defeat of Ravana and Goddess Durga’s victory over Mahishasura. Together, these stories reinforce the power of righteousness, courage, and moral duty.

Lord Rama and the Defeat of Ravana

According to the Ramayana, Ravana, the ten-headed king of Lanka, symbolised ego, arrogance, and misuse of power. Lord Rama fought Ravana to rescue Goddess Sita and restore dharma.

On Vijayadashami, Rama defeated Ravana, teaching that no matter how powerful evil appears, it ultimately collapses before truth and virtue. The burning of Ravana’s effigies symbolises the destruction of ego, lust, anger, and pride.

Goddess Durga and Mahishasura

In eastern India, Vijayadashami marks the day Goddess Durga defeated the buffalo demon Mahishasura, representing ignorance and oppression.

After nine days of battle, Durga emerged victorious on the tenth day, reinforcing the belief that divine feminine energy (Shakti) protects cosmic balance.

Deeper Spiritual Meaning

Vijayadashami symbolises inner victory — conquering one’s own fears, ego, and negative tendencies. It reminds individuals to choose righteousness even when the path is difficult.

The day is considered highly auspicious for new beginnings — education, business, art, and spiritual practices. In many traditions, children are introduced to learning on this day.

Regional Celebrations Across Bharat

  • North India: Ramlila performances and burning of Ravana effigies.
  • Maharashtra: Worship of Shami tree and exchange of Apta leaves.
  • Karnataka: Mysuru Dasara with royal processions.
  • West Bengal: Durga Visarjan and Sindoor Khela.
  • Himachal Pradesh: Kullu Dussehra celebrated for a week.

Key Rituals and Traditions

  • Ravana Dahan: Burning of effigies symbolising the end of evil.
  • Shami Puja: Worship of the sacred Shami tree.
  • Ayudha Puja: Worship of tools, books, and weapons in South India.
  • Seema Ullanghan: Crossing boundaries to start new ventures.

Food and Festive Practices

While Dussehra does not have a single fixed food tradition, families prepare sweets and festive meals to mark victory and joy.

  • Puran Poli
  • Kheer and Ladoo
  • Regional festive thalis

Dussehra is not merely a celebration, but a moral reminder — that righteousness always prevails, and inner discipline leads to true victory. It inspires individuals to uphold dharma in personal and social life.
