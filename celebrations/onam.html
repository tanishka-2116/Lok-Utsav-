Onam

The Festival of Prosperity, Equality, and Homecoming

Onam is the National Festival of Kerala and one of the most significant harvest celebrations in India. It is a ten-day festival that honours the legendary, kind-hearted King Mahabali, whose annual homecoming is believed to bless the land with prosperity, harmony, and happiness.

In 2026, Onam will be celebrated from 25 August to 4 September, with the most important day, Thiruvonam, falling on 4 September.

The Legend of King Mahabali

According to legend, King Mahabali was a just and benevolent ruler under whose reign Kerala experienced equality, abundance, and peace. There was no poverty, dishonesty, or discrimination in his kingdom.

The gods, threatened by his growing power, sent Lord Vishnu in the form of Vamana. Pleased by Mahabali’s devotion and humility, Vishnu granted him a boon — Mahabali could visit his people once every year.

Onam celebrates this symbolic return of a ruler who represents fairness, generosity, and good governance.

Cultural Significance of Onam

Onam marks the completion of the harvest season and reflects Kerala’s deep connection with nature, agriculture, and community life.

  • Celebrates prosperity and abundance
  • Promotes equality and social harmony
  • Strengthens family and community bonds
  • Honours tradition, art, and collective joy

Key Traditions and Rituals

Pookalam

During Onam, colourful floral designs called Pookalam are created at the entrances of homes. Made using fresh flowers, these designs grow more elaborate each day to welcome King Mahabali.

Vallam Kali (Snake Boat Race)

Vallam Kali is one of the most iconic Onam traditions. Massive snake-shaped boats race across Kerala’s backwaters, powered by hundreds of rowers chanting rhythmic songs, creating a breathtaking spectacle of unity and strength.

Pulikali

Pulikali, or the “Tiger Dance”, is performed mainly in Thrissur. Performers paint their bodies like tigers and hunters and dance energetically to loud drum beats, adding colour and excitement to the festivities.

Thiruvathirakali

Thiruvathirakali is a graceful traditional dance performed by women in a circle around a ceremonial lamp, symbolising devotion, elegance, and cultural continuity.

Traditional Attire

Onam attire reflects Kerala’s signature white-and-gold elegance.

For Women

Women traditionally wear the Kasavu Saree, a white or cream cotton saree with a golden border. Young girls often wear Pattu Pavada, a traditional silk skirt and blouse.

For Men

Men wear a Mundu (white dhoti with a gold border), paired with a kurta or simple shirt.

What Onam Teaches Us

Onam represents the idea of an ideal society — one based on equality, honesty, and shared prosperity. The legend of King Mahabali reminds people that true leadership is rooted in humility and service.

The return of Mahabali symbolizes the hope that good governance and fairness can still exist in the world. Onam encourages people to reflect on ethical living, social harmony, and respect for nature.

The Onam Sadhya

The Onam Sadhya is a grand vegetarian feast served on a banana leaf and is one of the most celebrated aspects of the festival. A traditional Sadhya may include 20 to 30 dishes.

Essential Dishes

  • Sambar
  • Avial (mixed vegetables with coconut)
  • Olan (ash gourd and beans in coconut milk)
  • Parippu (lentil curry)
  • Thoran (stir-fried vegetables with coconut)

Sides and Sweets

  • Upperi (banana chips)
  • Sarkara Varatti (jaggery-coated chips)
  • Pappadam
  • Payasam (sweet dessert made with milk, rice, or lentils)

Social Message of Onam

Onam carries a powerful message of equality, ethical leadership, and collective prosperity. It reminds people that true wealth lies in harmony, humility, and shared happiness.

Onam is more than a festival — it is a celebration of Kerala’s soul. Through flowers, feasts, boats, and dances, Onam brings people together to honour tradition, gratitude, and joy.
