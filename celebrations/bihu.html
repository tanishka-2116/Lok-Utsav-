Bihu

The Heartbeat of Assam’s Agricultural Life

Bihu is the most important and widely celebrated festival of Assam. Unlike many festivals observed once a year, Bihu is celebrated three times annually, each marking a different stage of the agricultural cycle.

Deeply rooted in nature, farming, and community life, Bihu reflects the rhythm of sowing, growing, and harvesting crops. It is a celebration of joy, gratitude, hope, and togetherness.

The Three Types of Bihu

Bohag Bihu (Rongali Bihu)

Celebrated in mid-April, Bohag Bihu marks the Assamese New Year and the arrival of spring. It is the most vibrant and festive of the three.

Homes come alive with music, dance, and celebrations. Young people perform Bihu dance, symbolizing fertility, youth, and the joy of new beginnings.

Kati Bihu (Kongali Bihu)

Observed in mid-October, Kati Bihu is a quieter, more reflective festival. This period coincides with a time when granaries are nearly empty.

Earthen lamps are lit in paddy fields and near the Tulsi plant to pray for the protection of crops and a good harvest. The festival reflects humility, patience, and hope.

Magh Bihu (Bhogali Bihu)

Celebrated in mid-January, Magh Bihu marks the end of the harvest season and coincides with Makar Sankranti.

It is a festival of abundance, feasting, and community gatherings, celebrating the fruits of hard agricultural labour.

Key Rituals of Magh Bihu

Meji and Bhelaghar

On the eve of Magh Bihu, people build temporary huts called Bhelaghar using bamboo, hay, and leaves. Communities gather inside these huts to enjoy a grand feast known as the Uruka feast.

The next morning, tall structures called Meji are burned as offerings to the Fire God, symbolizing purification and gratitude.

Bihu Dance and Music

The Bihu dance is one of the most energetic folk dances of India. It is characterized by brisk steps, rhythmic movements, and joyful expressions.

Traditional instruments include:

  • Dhol – Drum providing powerful rhythms
  • Pepa – Wind instrument made from buffalo horn
  • Gogona – Bamboo instrument producing buzzing sounds

Traditional Food of Bihu

Bihu celebrations are incomplete without Jolpan, a variety of traditional snacks and sweets made primarily from rice.

  • Pitha: Rice cakes such as Til Pitha (sesame-filled) and Sunga Pitha (cooked in bamboo).
  • Laru: Sweet balls made from coconut (Narikol Laru) or sesame (Til Laru) mixed with jaggery.
  • Chira and Doi: Flattened rice served with fresh curd and jaggery.
  • Uruka Feast: A community dinner enjoyed around bonfires on the eve of Magh Bihu.

Traditional Clothing

For Women

Women traditionally wear the Mekhela Chador, often woven from Muga silk, the golden silk unique to Assam. Red floral motifs are commonly used.

For Men

Men wear a cotton Dhoti and Kurta, accompanied by the iconic Gamosa, a red-and-white handwoven cloth worn around the neck or head.

Cultural and Social Meaning of Bihu

Bihu reflects the deep bond between humans and nature. It celebrates hard work, seasonal change, gratitude, and collective joy.

Through dance, food, rituals, and community life, Bihu preserves Assam’s cultural identity while welcoming new beginnings with hope.

Bihu is not just a festival — it is the soul of Assam. By honouring agriculture, nature, and togetherness, Bihu continues to connect generations through tradition, joy, and shared celebration.
