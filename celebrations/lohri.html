Lohri

A Festival of Fire, Harvest, and Punjabi Folk Joy

Lohri is a vibrant Punjabi folk festival celebrated every year on 13 January. It marks the end of the harsh winter and the beginning of longer days as the Sun begins its northward journey (Uttarayan).

Closely connected with agriculture, Lohri is a celebration of gratitude for a successful harvest, especially the Rabi crops such as wheat and sugarcane. The festival is deeply rooted in rural life, community bonding, and joyful expression.

Significance of Lohri

Lohri celebrates warmth, fertility, prosperity, and hope. The central symbol of the festival is fire, which represents the Sun, energy, and life.

  • Marks the seasonal transition from winter to spring
  • Expresses gratitude to nature and the Sun God
  • Celebrates agricultural abundance and rural prosperity
  • Symbolizes new beginnings and milestones in life

Key Rituals and Traditions

The Lohri Bonfire

The heart of Lohri celebrations is the community bonfire lit at sunset. Families and neighbours gather around the fire and perform Parikrama (circumambulation), offering prayers to Agni, the Fire God.

Offerings such as sesame seeds, jaggery, peanuts, popcorn, gajak, and rewri are tossed into the flames as a gesture of thanksgiving and hope for prosperity.

Dulla Bhatti – The Folk Hero

Most Lohri songs revolve around Dulla Bhatti, a legendary 16th-century Punjabi folk hero, often described as the “Robin Hood of Punjab”.

He is remembered for rescuing girls from slavery and arranging their marriages. The popular song “Sundar Mundriye” is sung in his honour during Lohri celebrations.

Celebrating New Beginnings

Lohri holds special importance for a child’s first Lohri or a newly married couple’s first Lohri. Such occasions are celebrated with great enthusiasm, gifts, and community feasts.

Music and Dance

The rhythmic beats of the Dhol bring Lohri celebrations alive. People gather around the bonfire to perform energetic folk dances.

  • Bhangra: Performed by men, expressing strength, joy, and celebration
  • Gidda: Performed by women, characterized by graceful movements and folk songs

Traditional Attire

Lohri attire is known for its bright, warm, and festive colours that reflect fire, harvest, and energy.

For Women

Women traditionally wear Patiala Salwar Suits, paired with vibrant Phulkari dupattas featuring intricate floral embroidery. Accessories include jhumkas, colourful bangles, and the traditional Paranda.

For Men

Men commonly wear Kurta-Pajama or Pathani suits, often complemented by a colourful turban (Pagri) and an embroidered waistcoat or Nehru jacket.

Footwear

Traditional Punjabi Juttis or Mojaris are worn by both men and women.

Traditional Food of Lohri

Lohri food focuses on seasonal winter ingredients that provide warmth, nourishment, and energy.

Main Course

The iconic Punjabi meal of Sarson ka Saag served with Makki di Roti and fresh white butter is central to Lohri celebrations.

Ritual Offerings (Prasad)

  • Til (sesame seeds)
  • Gajak and Rewri
  • Peanuts and popcorn

Sweets

  • Til Laddoos
  • Pinni (wheat-based sweet)
  • Jaggery-based preparations

Cultural and Social Message of Lohri

Lohri celebrates community bonding, gratitude, and joy. It reminds people of the importance of nature, agriculture, and shared happiness.

Through fire, music, dance, and food, Lohri brings warmth to hearts and strengthens social connections.

Lohri is more than a harvest festival — it is a living expression of Punjabi culture, resilience, and joy. As flames rise into the winter sky, Lohri ignites hope, prosperity, and togetherness for the season ahead.
