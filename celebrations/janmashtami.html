Janmashtami

The Divine Birth of Lord Krishna

Janmashtami, also known as Gokulashtami, celebrates the birth of Lord Krishna, the eighth avatar of Lord Vishnu. The festival commemorates the divine appearance of Krishna, who came to restore dharma and protect humanity from evil.

In 2026, Janmashtami will be celebrated on Friday, 4 September, followed by the energetic Dahi Handi celebrations on 5 September.

Spiritual and Cultural Significance

Janmashtami symbolizes the triumph of good over evil, love over fear, and devotion over ego. Lord Krishna’s life teachings — love, compassion, duty, and wisdom — continue to guide millions.

The festival is celebrated with fasting, prayer, singing devotional songs, storytelling, and joyful community gatherings.

Key Rituals and Traditions

Nishita Puja (Midnight Worship)

The most sacred ritual of Janmashtami is Nishita Puja, performed at midnight — the time when Lord Krishna is believed to have been born.

The idol of baby Krishna is bathed in milk, curd, honey, ghee, and water (Abhishek) and then placed in a decorated cradle or swing known as Jhulan.

Dahi Handi

Especially popular in Maharashtra, Dahi Handi recreates Krishna’s childhood love for butter. Teams called Govindas form human pyramids to break a clay pot filled with curd, butter, and prizes, hung high above the ground.

Raas Leela

In North India, particularly in Mathura and Vrindavan, Raas Leela performances depict episodes from Krishna’s life, including his divine dance with the Gopis.

Symbolic Footprints

In many South Indian homes, tiny footprints of Krishna are drawn using rice paste from the entrance to the puja room, symbolizing the Lord’s arrival into the household.

Traditional Attire

Janmashtami celebrations are marked by bright, festive, and joyful clothing.

For Children

Children are often dressed as Bal Krishna with yellow dhotis, peacock feathers, flutes, and forehead tilaks. Girls are dressed as Radha in colourful lehengas.

For Women

Women wear bright sarees or Anarkali suits in shades of yellow, orange, and blue — colours associated with Krishna.

For Men

Men traditionally wear dhotis or kurta-pajamas, especially during the midnight puja.

Traditional Food and Bhog

Since Lord Krishna was deeply fond of dairy products, Janmashtami food is rich in milk, butter, and curd. Devotees prepare special offerings called Bhog.

  • Makhan Mishri: Fresh white butter mixed with crystallized sugar, believed to be Krishna’s favourite.
  • Chappan Bhog: A grand offering of 56 food items, prepared in major temples like Mathura and Vrindavan.
  • Panjiri: A nutritious sweet powder made from wheat or coriander, nuts, and ghee.
  • Panchamrit: A sacred mixture of milk, curd, honey, ghee, and sugar.
  • Gopalkala: A savoury preparation made with puffed rice, curd, cucumber, and coconut, traditionally eaten after Dahi Handi.

Social and Cultural Message

Janmashtami celebrates innocence, devotion, joy, and divine love. It reminds people to live with righteousness, humility, and compassion, just as Lord Krishna taught through his life.

Janmashtami is not only the celebration of a divine birth, but also a festival that fills hearts with faith, joy, and togetherness. Through prayer, music, food, and play, Janmashtami continues to unite generations in devotion and celebration.
