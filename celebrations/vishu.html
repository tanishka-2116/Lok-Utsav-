Vishu

The New Year of Light, Hope, and Auspicious Sight

Vishu is the traditional New Year festival of Kerala, celebrated in mid-April when the Sun enters the zodiac sign of Aries (Mesha). The word “Vishu” comes from Sanskrit and means “equal”, symbolising balance, harmony, and renewal.

Vishu marks the beginning of a new agricultural cycle and is deeply connected to prosperity, gratitude, and positive vision for the year ahead.

Vishukkani – The Auspicious First Sight

The most important ritual of Vishu is Vishukkani, which literally means “the first thing seen on Vishu morning.”

On the previous night, elders prepare a sacred arrangement that devotees see immediately upon waking up, believing that the first sight of the year determines prosperity and fortune.

Items in Vishukkani

  • Lord Krishna idol or image
  • Golden yellow Konna flowers
  • Rice, fruits, and vegetables
  • Coins and gold ornaments
  • Mirror (symbol of self-reflection)
  • Traditional oil lamp (Nilavilakku)

Mythological and Cultural Significance

Vishu is closely associated with Lord Krishna, especially in temples like Guruvayur. Krishna represents divine playfulness, wisdom, and protection, making him central to Vishu worship.

The festival also aligns with solar movement and seasonal change, reflecting ancient Indian knowledge of astronomy and agriculture.

Traditional Vishu Feast (Vishu Sadya)

Food plays a central role in Vishu celebrations, reflecting the philosophy of balance in life.

Key Dishes

  • Vishu Kanji: Rice gruel prepared with coconut milk
  • Vishu Katta: A sweet dish made of rice flour and jaggery
  • Mango Pachadi: A blend of sweet, sour, and spicy flavours
  • Payasam: Sweet dessert made with milk and jaggery

Rituals and Celebrations

  • Early Morning Viewing: Family members are led with eyes closed to see Vishukkani first.
  • Vishu Kaineettam: Elders gift money to younger members as a blessing for prosperity.
  • Temple Visits: Devotees visit Krishna temples for blessings.
  • Fireworks: Children celebrate with crackers in the morning.

Traditional Attire

People wear new clothes on Vishu, symbolising renewal and fresh beginnings.

  • Women: Kasavu sarees (cream with gold border)
  • Men: Mundu with Kurta or shirt
  • Children: New festive clothing

The Deeper Philosophy of Vishu

Vishu teaches a profound lesson: what we choose to see first matters. By beginning the year with an auspicious vision, people train the mind to focus on positivity, abundance, and gratitude.

It is a celebration of hope, reminding us that life begins anew with every sunrise.
