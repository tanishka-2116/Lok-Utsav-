Holi

The Festival of Colours, Love, and Spring

Holi is one of the most joyful and vibrant festivals of Bharat, celebrated as the Festival of Colours. It marks the arrival of spring, the end of winter, and the triumph of good over evil.

Beyond colours and celebration, Holi represents renewal, forgiveness, unity, and the joyful expression of life. It brings together people of all ages and backgrounds, dissolving social boundaries through shared happiness.

When and Why Holi is Celebrated

Holi is celebrated on Phalguna Purnima, the full moon day of the Hindu lunar month of Phalguna, usually falling in late February or March. In 2026, Holi is expected to be celebrated around 3–4 March.

The festival symbolizes:

  • Victory of good over evil
  • Divine love of Radha and Krishna
  • Arrival of spring and renewal of nature
  • Forgiveness, reconciliation, and new beginnings

The Two Days of Holi

Holika Dahan (Chhoti Holi)

The night before Holi is marked by Holika Dahan, a ceremonial bonfire symbolizing the burning of evil. Families gather around the fire, sing, pray, and offer grains or coconuts.

This ritual is rooted in the legend of Prahlada and Holika, representing the protection of devotion and righteousness.

Rangwali Holi (Festival of Colours)

The main day of Holi is celebrated with colours, water, music, dance, and joy. People apply gulal (coloured powder), spray coloured water, and greet one another with laughter.

Homes welcome visitors with festive delicacies, making Holi a celebration of hospitality and togetherness.

Legends Associated with Holi

Prahlada and Holika

The most well-known legend behind Holi is the story of Prahlada, a devoted follower of Lord Vishnu. His father, King Hiranyakashipu, tried to punish him for his devotion.

Holika, Prahlada’s aunt, attempted to burn him in a fire, but divine grace saved Prahlada while Holika was destroyed. This story represents the eternal victory of good over evil.

Radha and Krishna

In the Braj region, Holi celebrates the playful and divine love of Radha and Krishna. According to legend, Krishna playfully coloured Radha’s face, beginning the tradition of playing with colours.

Kama and Rati

In Shaivite traditions, Holi is linked to the legend of Kamadeva, the god of love, who was burned by Lord Shiva and later revived. This legend symbolizes rebirth, love, and emotional renewal.

Regional Celebrations of Holi

  • Braj Region (Mathura–Vrindavan): Famous for Lathmar Holi and extended celebrations.
  • Maharashtra & Goa: Celebrated as Shimga or Shigmo with bonfires and folk traditions.
  • Punjab: Followed by Hola Mohalla, featuring martial arts and mock battles.
  • West Bengal: Celebrated as Dol Jatra or Basanta Utsav with music and dance.
  • Manipur: Observed as Yaosang, blending Holi with local dance traditions.
  • Nepal: Celebrated nationwide as Phagu Purnima.

Holi Food and Festive Delicacies

Food plays an essential role in Holi celebrations. Traditional sweets and snacks are prepared and shared to enhance the festive spirit.

  • Gujiya
  • Malpua
  • Mathri and Namkeen
  • Dahi Vada
  • Thandai (sometimes infused with bhang)

Cultural and Social Meaning of Holi

Holi encourages people to forgive past conflicts, renew relationships, and celebrate equality. During Holi, social barriers dissolve, reminding everyone of shared humanity.

In modern times, Holi also inspires eco-friendly celebrations, encouraging the use of natural colours and mindful enjoyment of the festival.

Holi is more than a festival of colours — it is a celebration of life, love, renewal, and harmony. Through laughter, colours, and togetherness, Holi reminds us that joy shared is joy multiplied.
