Mahashivratri

The Great Night of Lord Shiva

Mahashivratri, meaning “The Great Night of Shiva”, is one of the most sacred festivals dedicated to Lord Shiva, the supreme yogi and destroyer of ignorance.

It is observed on the 14th night of the waning moon in the Hindu month of Phalguna. In 2026, Mahashivratri falls on Sunday, 15 February, with the most auspicious Nishita Kaal Puja occurring from 11:55 PM to 12:56 AM.

The Deeper Meaning of Mahashivratri

Mahashivratri is not merely a festival, but a night of inner awakening. It symbolizes the overcoming of darkness and ignorance through awareness, discipline, and devotion.

Spiritually, this night is considered powerful because the planetary positions are believed to naturally raise the energy in the human spine. Staying awake (Jagran) with an upright posture helps seekers move towards higher consciousness.

A Sacred Story from Hindu Scriptures

One ancient legend associated with Mahashivratri comes from the Shiva Purana.

During the cosmic event of Samudra Manthan (the churning of the ocean), a deadly poison called Halahala emerged, threatening to destroy the entire universe. Neither gods nor demons could contain it.

Lord Shiva, out of compassion for all creation, consumed the poison to save the world. Goddess Parvati held his throat to stop the poison from spreading, turning it blue. Thus, Shiva came to be known as Neelkanth.

Mahashivratri is believed to be the night when Shiva performed this supreme act of sacrifice, reminding humanity that true divinity lies in selflessness and protection of life.

Key Rituals and Practices

Abhishek of Shiva Linga

Devotees perform Abhishek by bathing the Shiva Linga with water, milk, honey, and Panchamrit. This ritual symbolizes cooling Shiva’s intense energy and cleansing the devotee’s inner self.

Jagran (Night Vigil)

Staying awake all night while chanting, meditating, or singing bhajans is a central practice. Chanting “Om Namah Shivaya” is believed to purify the mind and soul.

Divine Marriage of Shiva and Parvati

Mahashivratri also commemorates the sacred marriage of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati, symbolizing the union of consciousness (Shiva) and energy (Shakti).

Traditional Attire

Dressing for Mahashivratri emphasizes simplicity, purity, and calmness.

  • Colours: Green (nature and balance), white and yellow (sattvic purity)
  • Men: Dhoti-Kurta or cotton Kurta-Pajama
  • Women: Sarees or Salwar Kameez in soft fabrics
  • Accessories: Rudraksha beads, believed to originate from Shiva’s divine tears

Traditionally, black and dark blue colours are avoided on this night.

Fasting and Sacred Food (Vrat)

Fasting on Mahashivratri is a spiritual discipline meant to detoxify the body and sharpen awareness.

  • Nirjala Vrat: Complete fast without food or water
  • Phalahari Diet: Fruits, milk, and non-grain foods
  • Popular Vrat Foods: Sabudana Khichdi, Kuttu Puri, sweet potato

Offerings to Lord Shiva include Bilva (Bael) leaves, Panchamrit, and seasonal fruits like Ber. Thandai is also traditionally consumed in many regions.

Marathi Mantra of Lord Shiva

This simple yet powerful Marathi mantra is commonly chanted on Mahashivratri:

ॐ नमः शिवाय ।
शिव शंकर कृपा करावी,
मन शुद्ध व्हावे, बुद्धी तेजस्वी व्हावी ॥

(Meaning: Salutations to Lord Shiva. May Shiva’s grace purify the mind and illuminate the intellect.)

Mahashivratri invites every seeker to pause, reflect, and rise above ego and ignorance. Through devotion, fasting, silence, and awareness, this sacred night becomes a gateway to inner transformation and spiritual strength.
