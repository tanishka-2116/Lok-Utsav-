Rath Yatra

The Sacred Journey of Lord Jagannath

Rath Yatra, also known as the Festival of Chariots, is one of the most iconic and globally recognized Hindu festivals. Celebrated annually in Puri, Odisha, it marks the grand procession of Lord Jagannath, along with his elder brother Lord Balabhadra and sister Goddess Subhadra.

During this festival, the deities travel from the Jagannath Temple to the Gundicha Temple, believed to be their maternal aunt’s home. In 2026, the main Rath Yatra begins on Thursday, 16 July.

Historical and Scriptural Background

Rath Yatra finds mention in ancient scriptures such as the Skanda Purana, Brahma Purana, and Padma Purana. Lord Jagannath is worshipped as a form of Lord Vishnu / Krishna, representing the universal Lord who belongs to all.

Unlike most temple rituals where devotees go to the deity, Rath Yatra is unique because the Lord himself comes out to meet the people, symbolizing divine accessibility, compassion, and inclusiveness.

Deeper Spiritual Significance

Spiritually, Rath Yatra represents the journey of the soul. The chariot symbolizes the human body, the reins represent the mind, and the wheels signify time and movement.

Pulling the chariot is considered an act of supreme devotion. It is believed that even touching the rope grants spiritual merit and liberation from past karmas.

The Three Sacred Chariots

Every year, three massive chariots are constructed from scratch using specific sacred wood, following age-old measurements and rituals. Not a single iron nail is used.

  • Nandighosa: Chariot of Lord Jagannath
  • Taladhwaja: Chariot of Lord Balabhadra
  • Darpadalana: Chariot of Goddess Subhadra

Major Rituals of Rath Yatra

Snana Purnima

Before Rath Yatra, the deities are bathed with 108 pots of sacred water. After this ritual, the deities are believed to fall ill and remain in isolation (Anavasara) for about 15 days.

Chera Pahanra

The Gajapati King of Puri performs Chera Pahanra, where he sweeps the chariots with a golden broom. This ritual conveys a powerful message: before God, all are equal.

Gundicha Yatra

The journey to Gundicha Temple signifies a break from routine life, allowing the Lord to mingle freely with devotees. The return journey is known as Bahuda Yatra.

Traditional Attire

Devotees traditionally wear Odia handloom clothing, reflecting simplicity and devotion.

  • Women: Sambalpuri or Ikat Sarees in red, yellow, and black
  • Men: Dhoti with Kurta or cotton vest
  • Priests: Dhoti with ritual Chadar

The deities themselves appear in various ritual costumes (Veshas), including the magnificent Suna Besha, where they are adorned with gold ornaments.

Traditional Food and Mahaprasad

The Jagannath Temple kitchen is among the largest sacred kitchens in the world, preparing food for thousands daily. The sacred offering is known as Mahaprasad.

Abhada (Main Feast)

  • Kanika (sweet aromatic rice)
  • Dalma (lentils with vegetables)
  • Besara (vegetables in mustard paste)

Ritual Sweets

  • Poda Pitha – Lord Jagannath’s favorite
  • Khaja
  • Rasabali
  • Malpua

The Lord is offered Chappan Bhog (56 food items) every single day.

Interesting Facts & Cultural Impact

  • The English word “Juggernaut” originates from Lord Jagannath.
  • Millions of devotees participate, making it one of the largest religious gatherings.
  • Rath Yatra is celebrated globally by the Jagannath diaspora community.

Rath Yatra is not merely a procession, but a powerful spiritual message — that the Divine walks among the people, accepts everyone equally, and guides the soul on its journey. It is a celebration of devotion, humility, and universal belonging.
